A payer's price is a multiple of Medicare.

Every plan's negotiated rate as a multiple of the public CMS schedule — and only where CMS actually pays that code standalone (Medicare OPPS payable-status codes for procedures & imaging, CLFS for labs; packaged/composite codes are never anchored to a bundle rate). A hospital sets a per-code price as a flat dollar (fee schedule), a cut of its list price (% of list), or a dollar with no method label (unlabeled) — for any one procedure a given plan uses one method >99.9% of the time, so each payer gets the single price it actually lands on, tagged with how. A taller bar = a pricier payer at this hospital. Each hospital's strip shows exactly how much of its file this view covers.

How to read this. One hospital, one service line at a time — a payer's multiplier varies by both, so pooling would mislead. Each payer bar is the median of its displayed plans' multipliers (each plan = its median × CMS across that line's codes, one median per code first), blended across however the hospital priced those codes, and split into the payer's distinct plans (many thin slices = a granular per-plan deal; one = a blanket rate). The tag says how most of that line's codes were set — fee schedule (flat dollar), % of list (a cut of the chargemaster), or unlabeled (a real published dollar whose method field is blank/"other") — every one is a published dollar shown as that dollar ÷ CMS. For payers priced through both lanes, the tooltip shows each lane's own median — the blend answers "what do they pay", not "which mechanism negotiated better".

What the ×CMS ruler is — and is not. The base is the national unadjusted CMS rate for codes Medicare pays standalone (OPPS payable-status; CLFS for labs). Composite/packaged OPPS codes are excluded, never anchored to a bundle rate. Arizona hospitals' actual OPPS payments run ≈6.6% above the national rate (FY2025 wage index 1.1095), so read ×CMS as a consistent ruler, not the exact local Medicare payment — and at critical-access hospitals (flagged in the strip) Medicare actually pays cost-based rates, so there it is a national ruler only.

What is excluded, and disclosed. Government programs (Medicaid/AHCCCS incl. MCO and abbreviated forms, Medicare Advantage, VA/TRICARE — decided per PLAN, so a gov-brand's genuinely commercial ACA/exchange products stay in), non-consumer payers (self-pay lines, corrections, workers' comp), payers outside the recognized brand set (counted per hospital as "not shown"), inpatient episodes (case-rate per admission, per-diem per day — a different unit, shown in the mix strip, never forced onto a per-code multiple), inpatient-setting per-code rows (the ruler is an outpatient schedule; counted per hospital), device/supply HCPCS codes (C/L-codes — components of a service, not shoppable services), codes without a standalone CMS anchor, plans with fewer than 12 priced codes (counted as "smaller plans" in the bar), rates outside 0.05–50× sanity bounds (counted per hospital), and self-contradictory listings — the same plan & code published at dollars ≥20× apart has no trustworthy single price, so the key is excluded and counted rather than laundered into a median. Psychiatric/LTCH facilities and one registry-duplicate file are excluded from this consumer view. Same-system campuses often publish one corporate fee schedule — the strip flags template-identical siblings. Nothing is trimmed by size within bounds — a genuine high or low rate is real signal. Source: byte-exact AZ hospital MRF captures × CMS OPPS Addendum B / CLFS 2025 schedules (the MRFs are CY2026 files — the ruler's vintage trails the rates by one cycle; a uniform, disclosed offset). Not comparable 1:1 with the CFT-explorer view on this site: that page measures multiples of each hospital's own recovered internal base (≈CMS × the hospital's markup), so its numbers run intentionally lower (median ≈1.5×, up to ≈5× on surgery) — same data, different ruler.
